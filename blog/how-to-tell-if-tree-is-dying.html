How to Tell If a Tree Is Dying: 7 Warning Signs

A quick guide to spotting trouble before a tree fails on your house — from a crew that removes dying trees every week.

1. Canopy Dieback from the Top Down

Healthy trees push new growth from the outside of the canopy inward. When you see bare branches at the very top of the tree while the lower canopy is still green, the tree is dying from the top. This is especially common in oaks suffering from bacterial leaf scorch or hypoxylon canker.

2. Fungal Conks or Mushrooms on Trunk or Roots

Hard, shelf-like fungal growths on the trunk — or mushrooms at the base — mean internal decay is already advanced. By the time you see a conk, the wood behind it may be substantially compromised. Have an experienced tree crew take a look before the next storm.

3. Bark Slipping or Sloughing Off

When bark starts falling off in large chunks and you can see bare wood underneath, the cambium (the living tissue just under the bark) is dead in that area. Small patches happen naturally in some species; broad sections are a serious warning sign.

4. Cracks or Splits in the Trunk

Vertical cracks, seams, or included bark in a fork (especially on big oaks and pecans) can lead to catastrophic failure — often in the next strong storm. When the crack runs deep, removal is usually the safer call.

5. Leaves That Don't Look Right

Watch for: premature fall color, yellowing between leaf veins, leaves smaller than normal, or entire sections of canopy with no leaves at all. Healthy North Alabama trees should hold full, green canopies through summer.

6. Fine Sawdust at the Base or in Bark Crevices

Fine, flour-like dust is often the sign of wood-boring insects — southern pine beetle in pines, emerald ash borer in ash, or flathead borers in stressed hardwoods. Pile this sign against other warnings and you have a clear picture.

7. Significant Lean or Soil Heave at the Root Flare

If a tree has developed a new lean or the soil on one side of the trunk has lifted or cracked, the root plate may be failing. This is one of the most dangerous signs — the tree can fall without further warning.

When Multiple Signs Stack Up

Any single sign above might not mean removal. Two or three signs together in the same tree usually does. Have an experienced crew walk the tree before deciding.
