Best Shade Trees for North Alabama Yards

A certified arborist's picks for what to plant (and what to skip) in Huntsville, Madison, and the Tennessee Valley.

Our Top 5 Shade Trees for Huntsville Yards

1. Willow Oak (Quercus phellos)

A tough, fast-growing native with fine leaves that shed cleanly. Reaches 60–80 ft with a graceful broad crown. Far less susceptible to bacterial leaf scorch than red oaks. Excellent street and yard tree.

2. White Oak (Quercus alba)

The classic. Slow to start but lives 200+ years. Resistant to oak wilt and most major diseases. If you plant one now, your grandchildren will thank you.

3. Bald Cypress (Taxodium distichum)

Deciduous conifer that tolerates both wet and dry sites. Clean, fine-textured foliage; beautiful gold fall color. No serious pest issues. Gets to 50–70 ft.

4. Sugar Maple (Acer saccharum)

Slower-growing and sturdier than its red and silver cousins. Gorgeous fall color. Performs well on the upper end of North Alabama heat (picks cooler pockets, Monte Sano, or the north-facing side of a lot).

5. American Hornbeam (Carpinus caroliniana)

Mid-sized native (25–40 ft) with smooth "muscle bark" and excellent fall color. Tolerates understory conditions where bigger trees shade out other options.

Trees to Avoid in North Alabama

1. Bradford Pear

Splits in spring storms, invasive, and kills nearby native tree seedlings. Please do not plant these.

2. Silver Maple

Fast-growing but weak-wooded. Aggressive roots crack driveways and clog drain lines.

3. Leyland Cypress

Common privacy screen that fails in middle age from canker diseases and storm damage. Consider Eastern redcedar or American holly instead.

4. Mimosa (Silk Tree)

Invasive and short-lived. Spreads aggressively into natural areas.

5. Red Oak in Droughty Sites

Red oak and pin oak are beautiful in the right spot but fail quickly from bacterial leaf scorch in stressed urban sites. White oak or willow oak is almost always a better pick.

Planting Tips

Dig a wide, shallow hole — never deeper than the root flare. Keep mulch pulled back from the trunk (no "mulch volcanoes"). Water deeply once a week for the first two summers. Don't stake unless the site is very windy — young trees develop stronger trunks when allowed to flex.
