Preparing Your Huntsville Trees for Tornado Season

North Alabama's peak severe weather runs March through May, with a secondary peak in November. Here's what to do before the next outbreak.

Huntsville Lives in Dixie Alley

The Tennessee Valley is one of the most tornado-active regions in the country. Since 1950, Madison County alone has recorded over 80 tornadoes. And tornadoes aren't the only threat — straight-line winds, derechos, and microbursts regularly exceed 60 mph. Every mature tree in your yard is exposed.

1. Get an Arborist Walk-Through Before March

A certified arborist can spot early signs of trouble — hollow sections, girdling roots, bacterial leaf scorch, weak unions — that homeowners miss. Doing this in January or February gives time to address issues before severe weather arrives.

2. Remove or Prune Highest-Risk Species

In Huntsville, the biggest storm-failure species we see:

  • Bradford pear — splits almost on schedule in spring wind
  • Loblolly pine — shallow root plate, topples in saturated soil
  • Water oak — prone to sudden limb drop, especially on older specimens
  • Silver maple — weak wood that shears
  • Tulip poplar — soft wood and tall height invites strikes

3. Correct Co-Dominant Leaders

Trees with two main trunks growing from the same point ("forks") are storm-failure magnets. Options: structural pruning to favor one leader, cabling to hold them together, or removing the tree before it fails.

4. Thin Dense Canopies

A tight, unthinned canopy catches wind like a sail. Proper thinning (no more than 25% per pruning) reduces wind load without harming the tree.

5. Remove Dead Limbs

Dead limbs ("widowmakers") are the most common storm hazard. They fall during every major event. A single deadwood pruning can eliminate dozens of these.

6. Check Root-Zone Conditions

Recent construction, trenching, grade changes, or compaction near a tree can destabilize it. If anything has happened within the drip line in the last few years, get an arborist opinion.

7. Prepare an Emergency Plan

Know who to call at 2 AM if a tree hits your house. Save (256) 555-0100 in your phone. Know where your insurance policy is. Know where the gas shutoff is.
