Who's Responsible When a Neighbor's Tree Falls on Your Property?

What Alabama law says, how insurance claims usually shake out, and how to protect yourself before the next storm.

Quick summary

In Alabama, the property where the tree falls — not where it grew — is generally responsible for cleanup and damage. Exception: if the owner knew the tree was hazardous (documented notice) and did nothing.

The Default Rule: "Act of God"

When a healthy tree falls during a storm, Alabama courts typically treat it as an act of nature. That means:

  • Your homeowners insurance handles damage to your property
  • Their homeowners insurance handles damage to their property
  • No one sues anyone over a tree that nobody could have predicted would fall

This holds even when the tree was physically on your neighbor's land but fell on your side.

The Exception: Known Hazardous Trees

If the tree was obviously dead, severely leaning, or already partially failed, and the owner was on notice of the hazard, they may be liable for negligence. This is where written documentation matters:

  • A formal written arborist risk assessment
  • Dated photos of the dangerous condition
  • Certified mail notice to the neighbor asking them to address the issue

Without this documentation, proving they "knew" is extremely difficult.

What to Do When a Tree Falls on Your Property

  1. Take photos of everything — damage, tree, and where it came from.
  2. Document the tree's condition if you can (was it obviously dead before? was there visible rot?).
  3. Contact your homeowners insurance to open a claim.
  4. Talk to your neighbor — they may file too since parts of the tree are on their land.
  5. Hire a licensed, insured tree service for removal. Get a written invoice for insurance.

What About a Tree Limb Hanging Over Your Yard?

Alabama follows the common-law rule: you may prune branches and roots from a neighbor's tree up to your property line, but not beyond. You can't enter their yard without permission, and you can't kill the tree by over-pruning (you may be liable for damages if you do).

Tips to Avoid the Whole Mess

  • If a neighbor's tree worries you, get a written arborist assessment
  • Send certified-mail notice with the assessment attached
  • Save a copy of everything
  • Maintain your own trees — it protects you from the reverse situation
